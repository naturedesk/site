code of conduct

NatureDesk rules for biodiversity intelligence, scientific collaboration, AI-assisted workflows, and external communication.

Version: 1
Date: June 1, 2026
Prepared for: NatureDesk
Primary reference: GEO BON, “Code of Conduct for (biodiversity and environmental) scientists”, May 3, 2023.

Scope: NatureDesk team members, digital colleagues, contributors, reviewers, partners, pilot users, and anyone using NatureDesk outputs in biodiversity, policy, planning, research, or reporting contexts.

Preamble

NatureDesk works at the boundary between biodiversity science, data infrastructure, policy support, and AI-assisted interpretation. That position creates a duty of care: toward people, toward nature, and toward scientific practice. This code adapts the GEO BON Code of Conduct to NatureDesk’s work with evidence, datasets, digital colleagues, partners, and decision-facing outputs.

The short standard is: respect people, respect nature, conduct science honestly, and never let technical speed outrun responsibility.

1. Overarching principles: we…

  1. Respect everyone and actively support equity, diversity, inclusion, and accessibility across age, gender, sexuality, race, origin, religion, ancestry and culture, social and economic status, physical and mental ability, language, discipline, and technical background.
  2. Respect nature by practising and promoting sustainable use of natural resources, minimising disturbance to living organisms and natural environments, and avoiding work that needlessly increases ecological risk.
  3. Conduct our science, analysis, and communication with honesty, transparency, impartiality, objectivity, intellectual rigour, and clear evidence trails.
  4. Use AI as an accountable support tool, not as a substitute for scientific judgement, consent, rights, review, or domain expertise.

2. Behaviours: we…

  1. Take time to be mindful, considerate, and supportive of colleagues, contributors, partners, communities, and users and their circumstances.
  2. Respect confidentiality, cultural sensitivities, Indigenous rights and sensitivities, local context, and partner constraints; we do not impose political, religious, institutional, or technological ideologies on others.
  3. Refrain from and actively oppose discrimination, harassment, bullying, intimidation, exclusionary conduct, and misuse of power.
  4. Respect the spirit and letter of relevant laws, regulations, agreements, permits, licenses, institutional requirements, and ethical frameworks; when these are inadequate, we work transparently for improvement rather than bypassing them.
  5. Practise integrity, honesty, impartiality, objectivity, and intellectual rigour in research, review, synthesis, software, data handling, and stakeholder communication.
  6. Remain accountable for mistakes and oversights in professional practice, publications, reviews, data collection, AI-assisted analysis, interpretation, and external briefings.
  7. Oppose misinformation and misleading presentation of evidence, including selective reporting that conceals biodiversity, social, governmental, industrial, scientific, or environmental issues that stakeholders should understand.
  8. Declare actual and potential conflicts of interest, including funding, commercial, institutional, policy, technical, or personal incentives that may influence our work or interpretation.
  9. Support the profession and colleagues by reviewing, mentoring, documenting, educating, sharing knowledge, improving workflows, and supporting those disadvantaged by breaches of this code.
  10. Acknowledge our backgrounds, assumptions, limits of expertise, and uncertainty in all relevant discussions, applications, and outputs.
  11. Foster the ideals of this code in others, including digital colleagues, staff, students, contributors, reviewers, and partner teams.

3. Practices: we…

  1. Consider the consequences our behaviour, tools, models, data processing, and advice may have for people and nature, including wildlife, habitats, climate, local communities, and institutions.
  2. Minimise health and safety risks to ourselves, other people, communities, field teams, and nature.
  3. Prioritise research, monitoring, AI, and data practices with low impact on people and nature, both in field settings and digital environments.
  4. Obtain permission from landowners, local communities, Indigenous Peoples, data stewards, institutions, and relevant authorities when fieldwork, data use, or knowledge use requires it.
  5. Make it clear when people are free to refuse participation, contribution, data sharing, or publication; explain relevant health, safety, confidentiality, data, and environmental consequences.
  6. Avoid spreading species, pathogens, pests, invasive organisms, sensitive location data, or harmful operational knowledge beyond appropriate boundaries.
  7. Deposit physical samples and reference materials in appropriate herbaria, museums, archives, repositories, or national collections where suitable, with rigorous metadata and permits.
  8. Maintain data sovereignty: partner, user-provided, Indigenous, local, confidential, restricted, or sensitive data must not be exported, republished, trained on, or exposed to external systems without explicit clearance.
  9. Keep NatureDesk workflows auditable. Significant outputs should preserve sources, assumptions, retrieval context, parameters, review state, and decision gates.

4. Publications, outputs, and external communication: we…

  1. Publish or share findings and supporting primary data accurately and in a timely way when permissions allow, so that work contributes to knowledge and enables transparency, reproducibility, and replicability.
  2. Acknowledge people, organisations, data providers, funders, reviewers, software maintainers, and prior work whose ideas, labour, or materials are incorporated.
  3. Respect intellectual and material property. Where we use third-party data, code, images, text, models, or local knowledge, we seek permission for use and release as appropriate.
  4. Respect sensitive data that could identify people, expose threatened species or vulnerable habitats, aid criminal or unethical behaviour, spread pests or pathogens, or infringe patents, copyrights, licenses, or confidentiality.
  5. Support ethical scientific publication and review even when findings conflict with our beliefs, interests, employers, clients, funders, or group consensus.
  6. Never fabricate or falsify findings, sources, citations, permissions, review status, model performance, or readiness labels; never plagiarise or condone misconduct by others.
  7. Follow recognised scholarly publishing and authorship guidance, including appropriate authorship, acknowledgements, contributor roles, citation, and correction practices.
  8. Write external NatureDesk material for the reader's practical decision: make thesis, evidence basis, uncertainty, limits, ask, burden, and next step visible early.

5. NatureDesk-specific AI and readiness rules

  • AI-assisted outputs are drafts or aids until reviewed. They may support extraction, classification, synthesis, drafting, coding, or checking, but they do not create authority by themselves.
  • Do not use AI to fabricate citations, simulate consensus, hide uncertainty, launder weak evidence, or make an output seem more reviewed than it is.
  • Protect restricted material from unauthorised external AI services. If the data cannot leave a trusted environment, the workflow must respect that boundary.
  • Keep internal readiness separate from external clearance. “Analyst-ready”, “citation-ready”, “user-facing”, “export-ready”, “policy-ready”, and “public-ready” must each have explicit meanings and gates.
  • If a readiness label is ambiguous or inconsistent with permissions, treat it as unsafe until clarified. Internal convenience must never become accidental public authorisation.
  • Before policy, client, public, or partner-facing use, require source traceability, rights clearance, uncertainty statement, relevant domain review, and accurate labelling of draft/prototype/reviewed/approved status.

6. Practical review checklist before external use

Before NatureDesk material is sent, published, demonstrated externally, or used to support decisions, the responsible person or bot should be able to answer yes to these questions:

  • Are the sources, data, methods, assumptions, and limits identifiable?
  • Are permissions, licenses, data sensitivity, Indigenous/local rights, confidentiality, and partner constraints respected?
  • Are uncertainty, sampling limits, geographic/temporal limits, and model limitations visible?
  • Has a relevant human/domain expert reviewed the material at the right level for its intended use?
  • Is the output clearly labelled as draft, prototype, internal, reviewed, approved, operational, or public-ready?
  • Could the output expose sensitive species, people, communities, locations, infrastructure, or confidential partner information?
  • Would a reasonable stakeholder understand what NatureDesk is claiming, what it is not claiming, and what decision it can safely support?

7. Concerns, corrections, and accountability

  • Raise concerns early when evidence is weak, a method is unclear, a source is unreliable, a readiness flag is misleading, or a result could be misused.
  • Correct mistakes transparently. Record what changed, why it changed, and which outputs may be affected.
  • Escalate serious concerns involving safety, harassment, discrimination, sensitive biodiversity data, personal data, legal/contractual restrictions, research integrity, or external misrepresentation.
  • No retaliation is acceptable against anyone who raises a good-faith concern or refuses participation under legitimate ethical, safety, cultural, or data-rights grounds.

8. Living document

This code should be reviewed as NatureDesk learns from pilots, contributors, reviewers, partners, and operational incidents. Updates should be versioned, dated, and briefly explained. Where NatureDesk collaborates with GEO BON, BONs, academic groups, Indigenous or local communities, public bodies, or commercial partners, this code should be read alongside their applicable codes, agreements, and ethical requirements.

Short form: Respect people. Respect nature. Be honest with evidence. Protect sensitive data. Credit contributors. Validate before external use. Never let AI speed outrun scientific responsibility.
